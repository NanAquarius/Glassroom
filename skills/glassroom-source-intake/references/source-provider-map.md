# Source provider map

Use this file when the task is to find **course-appropriate original materials** rather than just summarize whatever source the user already has.

## A. Diplomatic cables and foreign-policy archives

### 1. FRUS (Office of the Historian)
- URL: https://history.state.gov/historicaldocuments
- Best for: curated U.S. foreign-policy documents, historical diplomatic records
- Use when: the case involves historical U.S. policy decisions, diplomacy, negotiations, strategic judgments

### 2. U.S. National Archives — Foreign Affairs Research
- URL: https://www.archives.gov/research/foreign-policy
- Best for: archival research paths into official U.S. foreign affairs records
- Use when: you need archival provenance or want to move beyond secondary summaries

### 3. WikiLeaks PlusD / Cable collections
- URL: https://www.wikileaks.org/plusd/
- Best for: searchable diplomatic cables and cable-era primary texts
- Use when: you want raw diplomatic communications, subject-line searching, or case-building from cables
- Caution: treat as a document source, not as a blanket authority on interpretation

## B. Intelligence and declassified analysis

### 4. CIA Reading Room
- URL: https://www.cia.gov/readingroom/
- Best for: declassified intelligence reports, briefing documents, historical analysis
- Use when: the course task focuses on intelligence reasoning, assessments, or historical analytic framing

### 5. National Security Archive
- URL: https://nsarchive.gwu.edu/
- Best for: curated declassified collections, thematic dossiers, policy episodes
- Use when: you want higher-quality contextual framing around declassified records
- Note: some deeper collections point to DNSA / subscription resources

## C. Official statements and policy texts

### 6. Government / ministry / department sites
- Examples: State Department, DOJ, White House, EU institutions, UN bodies, national ministries
- Best for: official statements, press releases, position papers, policy announcements
- Use when: the assignment needs direct institutional language and not media paraphrase

### 7. International organizations
- Examples: UN, NATO, EU, World Bank, IMF, OECD
- Best for: resolutions, policy notes, official reports, institutional framing
- Use when: the case has international governance or multilateral dimensions

## D. Academic support layer

### 8. Semantic Scholar / OpenAlex / Google Scholar / library guides
- Best for: tracing scholarly context, identifying canonical sources, locating discussions around a primary case
- Use when: the primary material is unclear, incomplete, or needs framing for course writing
- Important: these are often support layers, not replacements for original source material

## E. Journalism and secondary analysis

### 9. Reputable journalism
- Examples: NYT, Reuters, AP, BBC, FT, WSJ, major investigative outlets
- Best for: timeline reconstruction, public framing, reaction mapping
- Use when: you need context around an event but still plan to anchor claims to stronger upstream sources

## Rule of thumb

If the goal is a course case, prefer:

- original communication
- official institutional text
- declassified records
- curated archival collections

Use journalism and commentary mainly to:

- orient
- cross-check
- find leads

not as the strongest evidentiary layer.
